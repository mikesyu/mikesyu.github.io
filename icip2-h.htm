Section 1               Background and Purpose

You are being asked to take part in a research study. This study will use an experimental drug called Study Drug to treat asthma. "Experimental" means that the drug has not been approved by any Authority that regulates new medicines, including the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA). This study is being carried out to evaluate the Study Drug in comparison with a placebo (a substance that looks like the Study Drug but is inactive). The purpose of this study is to see if the study drug, given as injections under the skin over approximately a year, can help control your asthma better when added to currently available asthma medications. Preliminary studies of this drug have shown an improvement in asthma control in those who received study drug. However, there is no guarantee that the drug will improve your asthma. There will be at least 1400 patients participating in this study. The study will be conducted at approximately 450 study centers in 20 countries worldwide.

The doctor overseeing this study, hereafter known as "study doctor," is being paid by Drug Company A (the company paying for the study or "the study sponsor") to carry out this study.

A couple of things to note:

1)     Whether or not you choose to take part in this study, you will still receive the medical care you already have been receiving.

2)     You may ask for a second opinion about your care at any time.

Please read the rest of this form carefully and ask the study doctor or the study staff if you have any questions.

Section 2               What Will Happen If I Take Part, and What Do I Have To Do?

If you agree to participate in this study you will first be enrolled. You will then undergo a series of screening tests, including a lung function test, to determine if you are eligible for the study. The screening phase will take one month and you will need to come to the clinic 3 times during this phase.

If you successfully pass the tests you will be assigned to one of two treatment options:

-                  Study drug every 4 weeks

-                  Placebo every 4 weeks

Which treatment you receive is decided at random by a computer (purely by chance, like the tossing of a coin). Thus, you have a 50 per cent chance of receiving the study drug. Neither you nor the study doctor will know whether you are taking the study drug or the placebo.

Assessments and procedures

After successfully complete the screening phase and being assigned to one of two treatment options, you will need to come to the clinic every 4 weeks.

At each visit your doctor will:

-                  Measure your vital signs

-                  Ask you to provide a blood sample for routine health measures

-                  Use a spirometry procedure to check your lung function. This involves blowing forcefully into a tube connected to a machine called a spirometer. The spirometer measures the amount of air you can breathe out in one second and the total amount of air you can breathe out.

-                  Give you an injection under your skin with either placebo or study drug.

You will also be monitoring two things daily at home:

-                  You will also be measuring your lung function at home using the peak flow meter. The home peak flow meter will measure how fast you can blow air out of your lungs in one breath. You will be asked to perform the test twice daily--in the morning and in the evening, before taking your regularly scheduled asthma inhaler.

-                  You will be provided with an electronic diary for you to use at home. You will need to record the results from the peak flow meter in your diary twice daily--in the morning and in the evening.

After your course of treatment is complete, you will need to come to the clinic one more time--two months after you finish taking the study drug. The purpose of this visit is to continue monitoring your safety after you finish taking the study drug.

When needed--e.g., if your asthma has worsened or you have experienced an adverse event--your study doctor might ask you to attend the clinic for an unscheduled visit to check your health.

Schedule of assessments/procedures

The planned length of time you will be in the study is 61 weeks, from your first visit to the final follow-up visit, and will include at least 17 visits to the clinic and 1 telephone contact.

The study visits are divided into five visit types. These are:

-                  Enrollment/screening: Visit 1 (week -4); Visit 2 (week -3) and Visit 3 (week -1)

-                  Treatment period: Visit 4 (week 0)-Visit 16 (week 44), treatment visits occur every 4 weeks; Visit 5 - phone contact

-                  End of treatment: Visit 17 (week 48)

-                  Follow-up: Visit 18 (week 56)

What do I have to do?

You must follow all instructions given to you about the use of other medications during the study and keep a record of any other medications that you take. In addition:

-                  You must refrain from donating blood and/or plasma from the time that you sign the informed consent and for 16 weeks after the last dose of study drug.

-                  Do not take any additional asthma medications without consulting your study doctor.

If you are a woman of child-bearing potential, you must use a highly effective method of contraception from Visit 1 throughout the study and for 16 weeks after the last dose of the study drug.

If you are a sexually active male, you must use an effective method of contraception from the first dose of the drug (Visit 4) and for 16 weeks following the last dose.

If you do become pregnant or learn that your partner has become pregnant during the trial, you should contact the study doctor as soon as possible.

Section 3               What Are The Possible Side Effects, Risks And Discomforts Of Taking Part?

The study drug or placebo is given as an injection under the skin. The study drug has caused side effects in some people, including the following:

-                  Decrease in white blood cell count

-                  Increased levels of white blood cell count

-                  Increased blood levels of creatine phosphokinase (a muscle enzyme)

-                  Increased blood levels of C-reactive protein (a protein that may indicate inflammation somewhere in the body)

-                  Protein present in the urine

-                  Common cold

-                  Headache

-                  Upper respiratory tract infection

Allergic reactions after an injection of the study drug are also possible and some reactions may be life-threatening. To ensure that you are not having a reaction, we will monitor you for two hours after each injection.

Your safety is our main concern. You will be closely monitored by a team of doctors and nurses, under the supervision of the study doctor, at every stage.

New Information

Your study doctor may receive new information about the study drug. If the study doctor believes it may affect your decision to take part in the study, you will be told. If this changes your decision to take part in the study, please talk with your study doctor about your decision.

Section 4               What Other Treatments Are Available?

You do not have to take part in this study to be treated for asthma. There are other approved treatments available for asthma. Your family doctor or the study doctor can explain the other treatments that are available.

Section 5               Costs and Payment

You will be reimbursed for normal out-of-pocket expenses incurred as a result of the visit, e.g., light lunch, teas/coffees and car-parking costs. Your site will collect your receipts from you at your visits. Details on the reimbursement process can be discussed with site staff. You will not be paid for lost pay or other losses. The study sponsor will not make any other payment to you, but you do not give up any legal rights by signing this form and may have other legal options.

There will be no cost to you for participating in the study. Additionally, if side effects or other physical injuries are caused by the study drug, the study sponsor will pay for reasonable costs of medical treatment that are not covered by your insurance or other programs.

Drug Company A--not you or your insurance company--will pay for the following items:

-                  Study drug

-                  Your quick reliever medication and your regular asthma inhaler (inhaled corticosteroid plus long-acting bronchodilator combination medications)

-                  Study visits

-                  Study procedures

-                  Study laboratory tests

Section 6               Disclosure of Health Information

You must give your authorization (permission) before the study doctor can use or share your health information with others. Some of the information that may identify you includes:

-                  Your address

-                  Your photograph

-                  Your date of birth

-                  Your social security number

If you sign this form, you allow the study doctor and employees to collect and use your health information to carry out this study. You also give your permission to disclose your health information to the study sponsor and its related companies, which are located around the world. These companies may use and disclose your health information to carry out the study and to apply for approval of the study drug, both in the United States and in foreign countries. The Institutional Review Board (IRB), a research ethics committee, that watches over the study, will also have access to your data.

The part of your health information sent by the study doctor to the study sponsor usually does not directly identify you (for example, by name, address, or social security number). However, if you sign this form it is possible that those persons may share your protected information with other persons. Federal law does not protect you against this, but the laws of your state may provide additional protection.

Section 7               Whom Should I Contact If I Need More Information Or Help?

You have the right to ask questions about this study at any time and are encouraged to do so.

-                  If you have any questions about this research study, ask the research study doctor or the study staff.

-                  If you experience an injury you believe is related to this study, contact the research staff or study doctor.

-                  If you have any questions about your rights as a research patient, contact the research staff or study doctor.

Section 8               Withdrawing from the Study

You may cancel your participation and consent at any time.To do this, you need to write to the study doctor at the following address: [Address]. You have the right to withdraw from this study at any time and still receive the same standard of care.

Note that The Sponsor would like to keep some of your blood, or the blood product specimens that are left over from the study, for future asthma related research. However, even if you decide now that your specimens can be used for future research, you can later request that they not be used for this research.

The study doctor may choose to end your participation in this study without your consent for any of the following reasons:

-                  You are unable to continue in the study

-                  You do not follow the instructions of the study doctor

-                  You experience an injury related to the study, or for any other reason.

If you stop taking part in the study completely, it is recommended that you go through the study withdrawal procedures that the Study Doctor considers necessary for your safety.
