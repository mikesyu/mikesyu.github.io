Section 2        What Is The Background and Purpose Of The Study?

This study will use an experimental drug to treat severe asthma. "Experimental" means that the drug has not been approved by any Authority that regulates new medicines, including the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA). The study is sponsored by Drug company a ("the Sponsor").

You have been asked to consider taking part in this study because you have severe asthma that is not well controlled. This study is being carried out to evaluate the study drug (as a medicine to treat severe asthma compared with placebo (an inactive substance). Preliminary studies in people with moderate-to-severe asthma have shown an improvement in asthma control in those who received study drug. The purpose of this study is to see if study drug, given as injections under the skin over approximately a year, can help control your asthma better if it is added to currently available asthma medications (eg anti-inflammatory inhaled steroid also referred to as inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) and a long lasting airway opener also referred to as long acting β-agonists (LABA)).

This study will explore how often the study drug needs to be given, who responds best to the study drug, and how well study drug is tolerated. This study will measure levels of the study drug in the blood and see how the body accepts the study drug.

There will be at least 1400 patients participating in this study. The study will be conducted at approximately 450 study centers in 20 countries worldwide.

Section 3        What Will Happen If I Take Part, and What Do I Have To Do?

If you agree to participate in this study you will be enrolled and undergo a series of screening tests to determine if you are eligible (suitable to take part) for this study. If you successfully pass these tests you will be assigned to one of the treatment options:

-        Study drug every 4 weeks

-        Placebo every 4 weeks

Which treatment you receive is decided at random by a computer (purely by chance, like the tossing a coin). This means you have a one in two (50%) chance of receiving the study drug. Neither you nor the study centre personnel will know which treatment you have received.

The study drug or placebo, will be administered by study centre personnel (medical doctor, nurse or other healthcare professional) by injection under your skin. It will be advised to you that the injection is made in a different location at each treatment visit (upper arm, thigh, or stomach).

Assessments and procedures

-        Medical and asthma history: the doctor will ask you about any previous and current medical conditions including the details of your asthma, and the treatments or medications that you are currently taking

-        Physical examination: an evaluation of the body and its functions by observing, palpation (feeling with the hands), percussion (tapping with the fingers), and auscultation (listening)

-        Weight and height measurement

-        Vital Signs: this includes measuring your blood pressure, heart rate, temperature, and respiration rate (how many breaths you are taking)

-        Electrocardiogram (ECG): this procedure records the electrical signals in your heart. It is done by placing sticky pads on your chest and limbs and produces a paper tracing

-        Blood sampling: you will be asked to provide blood sample for following assessments:

-   Routine check of your health (Visit 1, Visit 3, Visit 6, Visit 7, Visit 11,Visit 15, Visit 17) or early discontinuation Visit (IPD)

-   To check the status of your immune system Visit 4, Visit 7, Visit 11, Visit 15, Visit 17 or early discontinuation Visit (IPD))

-   Hepatitis B and C, and HIV (human immunodeficiency virus) tests (Visit 1)

-   Serum pregnancy test in women of child bearing potential; or to confirm that the women is not of child bearing potential by measuring hormone level (Visit 1)

-   To check of your allergic status (Visit 4)

-   To measure study drug level (Visit 4, Visit 6, Visit 7, Visit 9, Visit 11, Visit 13, Visit 15, Visit 17, Visit 18 or early discontinuation Visit (IPD))

-   To measure antibodies to study drug that may appear (an indicator of how well your body accepts study drug) (Visit 4, Visit 7, Visit 9, Visit 11, Visit 13, Visit 15, Visit 17, Visit 18 or early discontinuation Visit (IPD))

 Additional blood tests may be performed if your study doctor requires them for accurate evaluation of your health status or managing adverse effects.

-        Urine sampling: you will be asked to provide urine sample for routine assessment of your health and for performing urine pregnancy test in women prior to the study drug administration.

-        Spirometry: this procedure is to check your lung function. This involves blowing forcefully into a tube connected to a machine called a spirometer. The spirometer takes two measurements; the amount of air you can breathe out in one second (forced expiratory volume in 1 second, FEV1) and the total amount of air you can breathe out. The assessment will be performed before you take a bronchodilator (a substance that widens the air passages, to help more air get into the lungs). On particular visits (see schedule below) the measurement will be repeated after you receive a bronchodilator.

-        A reversibility test is an assessment to diagnose asthma and involves measuring FEV1 using spirometer before and after the inhalation of a bronchodilator. If you have had reversibility testing recently this screening test will not be performed. If you have never had this test or if it was long time ago (e.g. more than a year ago) the test will be done during screening. If the first attempt fails to show reversibility you may be asked to come to the study centre to repeat this test within 7 days after the first attempt. Any further procedures will be postponed until the reversibility testing is completed successfully.

-        If your lung function testing shows that you are eligible for the study, you will be provided with an electronic diary and a small hand held device known as a peak flow meter for you to use at home. Study Centre personnel will give you instructions on how to use these devices. The electronic diary will contain an Asthma Daily Diary and questionnaires about your asthma control (ACQ-6), quality of life (AQLQ(S)+12 and EQ-5D-5L), and how your asthma affects your ability to work, attend classes, and perform regular daily activities (WPAI+CIQ).

The electronic diary will be programmed to alert you and your study doctor when certain pre-specified asthma worsening thresholds are crossed based on your recorded asthma symptoms or lung function. When you receive such an alert you should contact your study doctor. You also should contact your study doctor (or your personal physician or urgent care centre if the study doctor is unavailable) if you feel that your asthma is worsening even if you did not receive an electronic diary alert.

You will be asked to complete the Asthma Daily Diary at home twice daily in the morning (before you take your regularly scheduled asthma inhaler) and in the evening (before you take your regularly scheduled asthma inhaler). The schedule for completion of the questionnaires is shown below.

-        You will be measuring your lung function at home using the peak flow meter. The home peak flow meter will measure how fast you can blow air out of your lungs in one breath. You will be asked to perform the test twice daily - in the morning and in the evening, before taking your regularly scheduled asthma inhaler.

-        Administration of the study drug: you will receive an injection of either study drug or placebo under your skin at each scheduled treatment visit (see schedule below).

On your first visit you will be asked to provide information about any medication you were previously taking or currently using.

Once you agree to participate in the study and provide your informed consent by signing this form, you will be asked to provide information about any adverse event (undesirable, unwanted, or unusual symptoms) you experience throughout the study participation. This information will be collected at each visit.

At each treatment visit you will be asked about your asthma, if there was any worsening in your condition and if you required any medical assistance. Your study doctor will also ask you about medications you are taking and if there were any changes since prior visit as well as about any adverse events you might have experienced.

Your study doctor will ask you about the health care services that are available to, and utilized by you. This includes the utilization of hospital resources, personal care and physician resources.

Your study doctor will contact you a week after your first treatment visit (Visit 4) over the phone to check how you feel, if you had any adverse events and if there were any changes in the medications you are taking.

Your study doctor might ask you to attend the clinic for an unscheduled visit to check your health when needed (e.g. when your asthma worsened or you had an adverse event). On these unscheduled visits your study doctor will perform additional testing required for evaluation of your health status.

Schedule of assessments/procedures

The planned length of time you will be in the study is 61 weeks, from your first visit to the final follow-up visit, and will include at least 17 visits to the clinic and 1 telephone contact. The study visits are divided into five visit types. These are:

-        Enrolment/screening: Visit 1 (week -4); Visit 2 (week -3) and Visit 3 (week -1)

-        Treatment period: Visit 4 (week 0)-Visit 16 (week 44), treatment visits occur every 4 weeks; Visit 5 - phone contact

-        End of treatment: Visit 17 (week 48)

-        Follow-up: Visit 18 (week 56).

Section 4        What Are The Possible Side Effects, Risks And Discomforts Of Taking Part?

Risks from study drug

The study drug may cause side effects.  You may experience none, some, or all of those listed below. The study drug used in this study may involve other risks that are not known at this time, including possible life-threatening reactions. There is always a risk involved in taking a new medication but every precaution will be taken and you are encouraged to report anything that is troubling you. Your study doctor will follow you closely for the entire time you are a part of this study. If you experience one of the side effects listed below or other new side effects, you should contact your study doctor right away and will be treated promptly with medicines that have the best chance of helping the side effects. If your asthma worsens significantly you may be treated in the manner you and your doctor feel is best.

Side effects that have been experienced by more than 15% of patients who have taken this drug include the follow:

-        Decrease in white blood cell count

-        Increased levels of white blood cell count

-        Increased blood levels of creatine phosphokinase (a muscle enzyme)

-        Increased blood levels of C-reactive protein (a protein that may indicate inflammation somewhere in the body), protein present in the urine,

-        Protein present in the urine

-        Common cold

-        Headache

-        Upper respiratory tract infection

Administration of protein drugs like study drug may result in the development of anti-study drug (also called anti-drug or ADA) antibodies. Potential risks associated with ADA include decreased drug efficacy and allergic reactions that can be life threatening. A study doctor will supervise the administration of the study drug and will observe you at the study centre for at least 2 hours after each injection; treatment will be immediately available if you have symptoms related to study drug administration.

There might also be unknown risks or side effects in humans that were not seen in previous studies.

New Information

Your study doctor may receive new information about the study drug.  If the study doctor believes it may affect your decision to take part in the study, you will be told. If this changes your decision to take part in the study, please talk with your study doctor about your decision.

Risks from placebo
Placebo does not contain active substance - study drug, but contains other substances, that can cause an allergic reaction.
Risks from other medication

You will continue to take your regular asthma inhaler and reliever medications that your primary (non-study) doctor prescribed for you.  Your Study Doctor can discuss the side effects of any of your regular asthma medications with you if you have questions.

The quick-acting bronchodilator (albuterol or salbutamol) that will be used for the best lung function testing is the same as your usual quick reliever medication for asthma. Albuterol/ salbutamol may cause the following side effects including slight muscle shaking, restlessness, headache and heart palpitation.

Risks from study procedures

The study drug or placebo is given as an injection under the skin of arms, thighs or stomach. Putting the needle under your skin may sometimes cause redness, swelling or pain where the needle is placed.

Obtaining blood may sometimes cause pain at the site where the blood is drawn from, bruising, occasional light-headedness and, rarely, fainting.

Performing the breathing tests (spirometry or home peak flow manoeuvre) may cause temporary mild chest tightening and coughing (worsening of asthma symptoms). Inhaled treatment with a fast acting airway opening medication can be used if necessary to open up your airway passages and help you breathe better.

ECG electrodes and gel may cause local irritation or redness.

Women Of Child-Bearing Potential and Men with Partners Of Child-Bearing Potential

There might be unknown risks to the unborn baby if you are or if you become pregnant during the study. Due to these risks, you must not take part in this study if you are pregnant, plan to become pregnant during the research study period, or are breast-feeding a baby.

The effects of study drug on the unborn fetus are not known. It is very important that you practice birth control to prevent pregnancy during this study. Women who are pregnant or nursing mothers are not allowed to be in the study as the safety of the study drug in pregnancy and to nursing children is unknown. If you become pregnant during the study you will not be given any more study drug.

The effects of study drug on male sperm formation are also unknown. While the possibility of affecting sperm is unlikely it is still important that men use an effective method of contraception or practice abstinence from the first dose of study drug until 16 weeks after last dose.  Your doctor or the study staff will review effective methods of contraception with you.

If you are a woman of child-bearing potential:

-        By signing this consent form, you confirm to the best of your knowledge that you are not pregnant now and you do not intend to become pregnant during this study

-        A pregnancy test will be done to confirm that you are not pregnant before you take part in this study

-        You must use highly effective method of birth control, , from the first visit, throughout the study and  for16 weeks after last dose of study drug.

If at any time during this study you think you might be pregnant, or later learn that you were pregnant during the study, you must contact the study doctor immediately for further instructions about your participation in this study and follow-up.

If you are a man with a partner of childbearing potential, then you must immediately contact the study doctor if any of the following occur:

-        Your partner becomes pregnant during the study

-        You learn that your partner became pregnant during the study

-        You learn that your partner became pregnant up to 16 weeks after your last dose of the study drug.

You will be given further instructions for follow-up.

The study doctor may ask you about any pregnancy during your study visits, and again up to 16 weeks after the last dose of study drug.  If you report a pregnancy of your partner, the study doctor will:

-        Collect information about the results of the pregnancy/birth

-        Schedule any follow-up visits that are needed.

This health information will become part of the research study records.  It will be shared with the study sponsor.  With this information, the study sponsor may be able to determine if there are any effects of the study drug upon unborn children.
